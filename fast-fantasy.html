Fast Fantasy

version 1.1, by David Bass

What is this?

This is a lightweight, streamlined tabletop fantasy roleplaying game. This game is most directly inspired by:

  • Apocalypse World (Vincent Baker and Meguey Baker)
  • Dungeon World (Sage LaTorra and Adam Koebel)
  • World of Dungeons (John Harper)
  • Defying Danger (Jeremy Strandberg) spoutinglore.blogspot.com

The goal of this TTRPG is to provide an easy-to-use framework for new players to get a game session up and running quickly and to improvise an adventure.

Printing and Materials

Print these documents on 8.5‚Äù x 11‚Äù (or A4) paper, landscape and single-sided. You‚Äôll find that it contains:

  • A page with advice on being a player

You‚Äôll want some pencils and erasers, some extra scrap paper, and at least 3 standard, six-sided dice (üé≤üé≤üé≤). Ideally, you want 3 dice per person.

For 2-7 friends over 3-5 hours

Get together with at least one friend, up to 6 friends (plus you) max. The game works best with 5 people total. Plan on playing for at least a couple hours, most likely 3-5.

Decide who will be the game master, or GM. Everyone else is a player. Each player controls and portrays one fictional player character (or PC) ‚Äî a Warrior, a Scoundrel, a Mystic, or a Wildsoul. The GM controls and portrays the imaginary world around them, having the world act upon the PCs and reacting to what they do.

The Game is a Conversation

You play this game by talking to each other. You‚Äôll say something, they respond. You‚Äôll ask/answer questions, interrupt, or talk over each other. You take turns, but there‚Äôs no strict, set turn order; just make sure everyone gets their chance in the spotlight, like in a theater play.

The goal of this conversation is to create the fiction, a shared imaginary space in which the PCs exist and act.

The players are responsible for their characters: what they do and say, how they feel, their backgrounds and experiences prior to play.

The GM is responsible for everything else: the world, the non-player characters (NPCs), the monsters, etc. They describe how the world responds to the PCs‚Äô actions, and they curate what‚Äôs true about the world. The GM can and should ask the players for input and ideas, but the world beyond the PCs is ultimately the GM‚Äôs domain (an idea known as The Line).

A Note on Safety at the Table

Roleplaying with your friends can be lots of fun, but it‚Äôs important to keep in mind everyone‚Äôs boundaries and preferences. Since it can be tough or awkward to try and stop in the middle of the action, it‚Äôs a good idea to discuss beforehand what sort of content everyone is on board with and what content needs to be excluded, veiled, or handled carefully before the game begins. Safety always takes priority over the dice, the rules, and the story. For more information and some great tools for handling content safety during play, check out this or that link.

Getting Started

For your first time playing, pick an adventure starter that everyone is excited about. The adventure starter will:

  • Establish a premise
  • Guide you through creating and introducing the PCs
  • Prompt you to establish motives, goals, complications, details, etc.
  • Help hook the PCs into the fiction

Adventure starters might also include (for GMs) a map, dangers the PCs might face, and discoveries they might make.

The Flow of Play

The GM frames a scene ‚Äì they say (or ask) where the PCs are and what that place is like. They say (or ask) who else is there, what time it is, and so forth.

The GM establishes the situation, saying (or asking) what‚Äôs going on and providing more details to the players.

The GM says something to provoke action and/or increase tension. Then they ask ‚ÄúWhat do you do?‚Äù

The Flow of Play, Continued

The players will either ask for more info or say what they do (as their characters, talking in 1st or 3rd person is fine).

  • The GM answers questions and clarifies the situation
  • If the player triggers one of their moves (Take the Risk, Wade Into Battle, Manipulate, Get Answers, Lead Onward, or another move), they work with the GM to resolve it.
  • Otherwise, the GM says what happens based on the fiction.

The process then repeats: the GM establishes the situation, provokes action or increases tension, and asks ‚ÄúWhat do you do?‚Äù If the scene ends, the GM frames the next scene. If they‚Äôre not sure how to frame the next scene, they ask the players questions until they are sure.

The flow of play is conversational. There are no formal turns, even during fights. Players can and will interrupt each other and interject (but please be respectful and give everyone else a chance in the spotlight). What‚Äôs possible, plausible, or likely is something to be decided and negotiated by each group.

The GM doesn‚Äôt roll to act for NPCs, monsters, or the world. (The GM may roll to see how much harm an NPC or monster inflicts, or they may have the player roll for the harm amount instead; either way is fine.) Instead, the GM describes how the world and its denizens act upon the PCs and then gives the PCs a chance to react. If they ignore the threat, act foolhardy, or roll poorly, then the NPC may get what they want, the enemy‚Äôs attack may hit, or some other consequence may befall the PC, depending on the GM Move the game master decides to make.

A guide to Playing a Character

Your Agenda
  • Be a fan of the other players, the GM, and the world
  • Portray a compelling character
  • Engage actively with the fictional world
  • Play to have fun together, not to win
Principles
  • Share the spotlight with other players
  • Be bold, take risks, and embrace what comes after
  • Pay attention, ask questions, and offer suggestions
  • Help build the world, but defer to the GM if needed
  • Embrace difficulty, setbacks, and failure
  • Show us what‚Äôs important to your character
  • Describe things through your character‚Äôs eyes
  • Make connections with other characters
  • Build off of what the GM and other players say
  • Think in - character, but be considerate of others
  • Begin and end with the fiction
When In Doubt
  • Visualize the scene, consider the situation
  • Ask the GM for clarification
  • Think about what you want & your character‚Äôs goals
  • Consider your character‚Äôs strengths and flaws
  • Ask around for ideas, but you choose what to do
  • Don‚Äôt shy away from the obvious choice, the brash choice, the interesting choice, or the meaningful choice. Remember, you get XP if you roll a 6-!
Make Your Move
  • When you want to do something, don‚Äôt jump straight to rolling the dice. Converse with the GM about which move you‚Äôre making, and for Take the Risk, work with them to establish the gains and costs.
  • If what your character does in the fiction seems to trigger a move, then it does. If you want to make a move, you‚Äôve got to actually do the thing in the fiction. Work it out with the GM, but remember you can always change your mind and back out of it (before you roll, that is).
  • Describe the how of the actions your character takes, not just the what. You don‚Äôt need to go into great detail, just enough that everyone can picture it in their minds. Tie things back into the established fiction.
  • Don‚Äôt worry too much about trying to make things fit your chosen specialties when you Take the Risk.
Creating Your Character

First off, your character needs to be conducive to both the group you‚Äôre playing with and the established fictional world. The GM should give you some kind of prompt to go off of before everyone makes their characters, so make sure to create a character that is actually going to go along with everyone on your shared adventure. The players will probably have more fun if they diversify their character types, but don‚Äôt worry too much if you and another player choose the same type.

  1. Pick a character type, choosing from Warrior, Scoundrel, Mystic, or Wildsoul.
  2. Pick a name and look (you can reference the People, Places, and Possibilities page for inspiration on names).
  3. Decide for your character what‚Äôs important to them and their personal goals. Choose things that you can demonstrate while roleplaying during the adventure.
  4. Each player asks Which one of you... to at least one other player. These help establish past connections between characters and reasons you‚Äôre in the group.
  5. Each player picks options for Take the Risk. These are areas of expertise that your character excels at . You can ask the GM and other players for input if you want. Then everyone reveals what they picked.
  6. Each player picks options for Mettle / Cunning / Power / Wisdom. These are things your character can do with certainty, or supernatural abilities that they can perform. Ask for input if you want , and reveal.
  7. Each player picks Inventory and Little Things. You can check the Adventuring kit boxes and decide what you need later. Make sure you have Supplies to share!

Maybe you want to play a character a bit more unusual, like a half-dragon or a psychic hive-mind of spiders. As long as the GM is okay with it, go for it! Keep in mind though that you‚Äôre playing in a group, so you‚Äôre not the main character, you‚Äôre one of the main characters. If you want to be able to do something an ordinary person wouldn‚Äôt be able to do, that‚Äôs a perfect opportunity to use the write-in options for your Take the Risk skills or things you can spend Mettle, Cunning, Power, or Wisdom to do. Work with the GM to figure out the specifics, and remember that limitations can be fun!

Taking the Risk

Take the Risk is the most common move your character will make. Discuss with the GM what you‚Äôre trying to accomplish and what things might go awry, even if you succeed. These could be a complication, a downside, an additional cost , or maybe you aren‚Äôt able to get quite all of what you want , at least in one attempt . If you aren‚Äôt happy with the stakes that you and the GM set (before you roll the dice), you can always decide to have your character take a different action instead.

Mettle, Cunning, Power, and Wisdom

Though these points have different names for each character type, they all function the same. When you spend a point, you just do the thing, so long as it is reasonable. If it‚Äôs asking a question, the GM will answer honestly. The exception is for magic, which requires a roll.

Getting Hurt and Recovering

When your character would take harm, you decide how to deal with it. Reduce it by your armor, maybe spend a point, or even trigger consequences in the fiction. Mark harm boxes top to bottom, but sometimes the GM may have you mark a specific harm box instead.

Supplies and Having What You Need

Supplies are some of the most important equipment an adventurer can carry. You can just so happen to have almost any reasonably-sized, mundane item in your pack by using Supplies. Supplies are most often used for:

  • Having what you need by expending 1 Supplies if the item can be comfortably held in 1 hand, or 2 Supplies if the item would need 2 hands to hold or use properly.
  • Regaining 1 Mettle / Cunning / Power / Wisdom when you stop to catch your breath and use 1 Supplies, or regaining all your points when you get a few hours of rest and use Supplies equal to the number of people resting plus 1. This allows each resting PC to recover.
  • Clearing the Injured harm box when you take the time to treat the injury and expend 1 Supplies, or clearing the Debilitated harm box when you get a few hours of rest and expend 1 Supplies.

Don‚Äôt bother marking off a use of Supplies if the item is fairly small or inexpensive. Refer to the sidebar on the page of Artifacts : Trinkets, Talismans, and Treasures for more examples of things you can have as Supplies.
